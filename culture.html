Cultural Guide

Culture & Etiquette

Japan's culture rewards visitors who arrive prepared. A few essentials before you land.

Remove shoes at temples & homes

Look for the step at an entrance (genkan) and the row of shoes beside it. Follow suit — removing shoes before entering a home or traditional space is non-negotiable.

Quiet on the train

Tokyo's trains are remarkably silent. Avoid phone calls, mute your device, and refrain from eating. Many lines have designated quiet carriages.

Cash is king

Many restaurants, shrines, and smaller shops are cash-only. Always carry yen. 7-Eleven, Lawson, and Japan Post ATMs reliably accept foreign cards.

Bow as a greeting

A small bow goes a long way — any sincere effort is appreciated and warmly returned. Handshakes are fine with international contacts, but lead with a bow in traditional settings.

No eating while walking

Eating on the go is generally considered impolite. Find a nearby bench or step and eat there. Festival food at matsuri events is the main exception.

Get a Suica or Pasmo IC card

Tap in and out on every train and bus; works across virtually all Tokyo transit. Also accepted at convenience stores, vending machines, and many restaurants.

Download offline maps

Mobile data is reliable in Tokyo, but offline maps are essential for navigating subway dead zones. Download before you land.

Two hands for giving and receiving

Present and receive objects — business cards, gifts, dishes, money — with both hands. Place cash on the tray at cashiers rather than directly in the hand.
