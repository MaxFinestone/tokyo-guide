Seasonal Guide

When Tokyo Turns Pink

Everything you need to know about sakura season — from the best viewing spots to when to arrive.

Cherry blossom season — sakura — is Japan's most celebrated natural phenomenon. For roughly two weeks each spring, Tokyo's parks, streets, and canal paths are transformed under an explosion of pale pink bloom.

When Does Sakura Season Happen?

Tokyo's cherry blossoms typically reach full bloom (mankai) in late March to early April, though exact dates shift by 1–2 weeks each year. The Japan Meteorological Corporation publishes annual forecasts from January onwards. Peak bloom lasts only 5–10 days before petals fall (hanafubuki — “flower snowstorm”).

The Best Viewing Spots

Shinjuku Gyoen — Over 1,000 trees of 65 varieties stagger the bloom across weeks. Alcohol-free, calm, and meticulously maintained.

Ueno Park — 1,000 cherry trees line the main promenade in a tunnel of pink. Famous for hanami parties; arrive at dawn for the serene pre-party atmosphere.

Chidorigafuchi — Rowboats drift beneath overhanging cherry branches along this palace moat. The most romantic sakura experience in Tokyo.

Meguro River — Cherry trees line both banks for 4 km. At night, lanterns illuminate the scene in pink and gold. Best walked from Nakameguro station.

Hanami — Flower Viewing

Hanami (花見) is the tradition of gathering beneath cherry trees to eat, drink, and appreciate the transience of beauty. The tradition reflects mono no aware — a wistful awareness of the impermanence of beautiful things.

  • Book accommodation as soon as the forecast releases — March and early April are peak season.
  • Shinjuku Gyoen bans alcohol; Ueno Park and riverside spots do not.
  • Higher elevation spots bloom 1–2 weeks later.
